Video

The present section aims to introduce the reader into those main projects in relation to video technology where I have worked on. It is a very simplified overview designed to make everybody understand what they are about (of course, much more could be said about).

Simplified architecture

One of the main applications of video technology is the transmission of real content to viewers. For this simplified architecture this end-to-end scenario is used: the video content goes through different steps from the moment a scene is captured with a camera to the moment when the final user consumes the video content. I highlight the word simplified because several business and technical processes can be attached to the presented architecture in order to offer not only required services or products but extra ones. For instance take into consideration sport events or news: the audiovisual content at origin may not only be transmitted but enriched and served in different ways.

We can split this simplified architecture in three networks that will be described next: contribution network, distribution network and delivery network. Each of these networks has its own particularities (applicable telecommunication technologies and processings, levels of quality, reliability or security, etc.). For the sake of simplicity, just consider these networks are connected by specialized entities (e.g. production studio, data center, ...). From a service perspective, the aim of the whole system is to offer audivisual content consumption within acceptable levels of quality of experience.

Contribution network

Contribution mission is to deliver the highest feasible quality within budget to the reception headend of a processing entity (tipically a production studio).

The content will suffer many degradation processes afterwards, so in order to avoid that content reaches inadmissible levels of quality it is basic that the original signal is transmitted with very good quality at the beginning of the end-to-end flow. For digital video, information lost at sampling and compression-decompression cycles (multigeneration) are two of these main degradation processes, as the raw video signal cannot be transmitted to the viewer, stored or processed as it is. Other important attributes are:

  • Security: to protect original content from disclosure and unauthorized use.
  • Reliability: to guarantee every piece of information to transmit is actually conveyed (redundant links are usually placed for this).
  • Timeliness (depending on the kind of program): to ensure the information arrives on time (e.g. live events, live bi-directional conversations, ...).

Contribution links use specific telecommunication technologies appropriate for the setup, currently satellite, fiber and baseband (SDI) are quite common.

Distribution network

Its function is to transmit finished content from final production centres to contracted content delivery providers. Delivery providers are who eventually send the content to the viewers and hence involve network operators, over-the-air broadcaster, satellite companies, cable companies, etc.

Different versions of the content may flow to one or several delivery providers and besides the content service information is included, creating a digital multiplex of information (content, signalling, etc.).

At distribution it is assumed that no more multigeneration will happen and then content is adapted to fit the bandwidth requirements of the delivery network, which implies to reduce the information of the audiovisual signal more aggressively in the distribution link than those used in the contribution link by means of compression and sampling. However, quality is still a significant concern, as well as security, reliability, cost and timeliness.

Delivery network

Its function is to transmit the content to the viewers by means of the technology that binds the delivery provider and multiple viewers (almost always many). Examples of widespread deployed technologies for delivery networks are:

  • Terrestrial radio links.
  • Terrestrial cable links.
  • Fiber links.
  • Satellite links.

Each one of these technologies has its advantages and drawbacks. All the previous features are still important but optimizing cost becomes more significant.

The delivery provider can decide to further process the content stream (e.g. storage, ad insertion, etc.). For instance, the IP-based video delivery networks of network operators employ systems based on Content Delivery Networks (CDNs) that allow to apply specific business models, like for example video on demand, recording, timeshift, ad insertion, blackout, personalization, etc.
